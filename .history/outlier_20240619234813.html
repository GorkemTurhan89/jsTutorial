Things to ALWAYS correct in every selected response (complete a rewrite):

  • Formatting. Would the response be better as bullet points? Change it!
  • Unnatural word selection. Did the model say something that sounds odd in your language? Change it!
  • Grammatical errors. Did the model make a grammar mistake? Fix it!
  • Pleasantries. Is the model saying Sure! Thanks! Or other spambot phrases? Remove them!
  • Hallucinated content. Did the model make something up that isnt true? Fix it!
  • User satisfaction. Did the model completely answer the users prompt? No? Fix it!

KEYS FOR EVALUATING ACCURACY:

  1. Check each fact in the response via Google and verify that it is true
  1. Watch out for model hallucinations (the model treating false facts as if they are real)
  2. For facts that are unverifiable or subject to change (like prices), unless a specific date is mentioned, adopt the most up-to-date information
  3. There might be times that the models will make up locations check for those.
  4. Check for accurate information on dates and years. For example, the model might say Abraham Lincoln is the 15th U.S. President when he is in fact the 16th.

TONE GUIDANCE

Be sure the selected responses tone is:

  • AVOIDING PLEASANTRIES. Delete any language such as happy to help or let me know if there is anything else I can help with
  • Neutral. Ensure your tone doesnt express any particular attitude or emotion, unless requested by the user.
  • Balanced. Ensure you consider multiple viewpoints in your response, unless requested otherwise by the user.
  • Natural. Use expressions and phrases that are used by native speakers in the target language, and ensure the response does not read awkward.
  • At the appropriate expertise level. If the user doesnt sound like an expert, based on the prompt, be sure to use the appropriate language.
  • Empathetic. If the user is asking questions about their troubles, be sure to respond in an empathetic manner. Take care not to come off as patronizing or preachy.
  • Using the active voice. My homework is done vs My homework is being done
  • Varying sentence structure. Use varying sentence lengths and structures to make the response sound natural.

Be sure your response is formatted well. Use bullet points when appropriate, and always rewrite if the exisitng formatting is not ideal.

If the selected response does not exhibit the proper tone, you must complete a rewrite.

A vs B Response Rating Cheat Sheet

The First sentence of the Justification must convey which response is better than the other in the following way:

Response X is (Much Better, Better, Slightly Better, Negligibly Better) than response Y

Where:

  • 1 - Response A is much better than Response B
  • 2 - Response A is better than Response B
  • 3 - Response A is slightly better than Response B
  • 4 - Response A is negligibly better than Response B
  • 5 - Response B is negligibly better than Response A
  • 6 - Response B is slightly than Response A
  • 7 - Response B is better than Response A
  • 8 - Response B is much better than Response A

After that first sentence, we need to explain why we chose one response over the other,


EXAMPLE: Response A is negligibly better than Response B, because it provides a more detailed exploration of the ideological and strategic differences between Voldemort and Sauron. Response A includes specific examples, such as Voldemort's use of Horcruxes and Sauron's creation of the One Ring, which help to deepen the reader's understanding of their methods and motivations. Additionally, Response A discusses the potential tensions and points of friction in a possible alliance between the two villains, offering a more nuanced analysis. While Response B covers similar points, it lacks the depth and specificity found in Response A.

How to stay enabled:

  • Ensure your task is consistent. If you say Response A is better, your justification, likert score, and review must match.
  • Ensure you search responses for accuracy errors. If found, identify them in the justification text and complete a rewrite.
  • Whenever you find a mistake in the selected response, be sure to complete a rewrite and fix that mistake. Mark down the appropriate rating as well.
  • Complete a rewrite and mark down fluency if there are English words in the selected response.
  • Write a prompt in the appropriate category with appropriate complexity.

Cheat Sheet to Do Tasks Quickly

Here is a guide to help you get through tasks as quickly as possible. Flamingo Preference Ranking Cheat Sheet

The goal of this sheet is to provide tips and tricks to expedite the tasking process. The parts of the task that take the most time are: prompt creation, reading through responses, fact checking, and passing the grammar checks. The tips provided should give you some tips as to how to get through these parts as quickly as possible.

Step 1: Prompt Creation

Create an initial prompt for an interaction with a chatbot. You will need to write your prompt:

  • In your local language
  • Within a certain category + subcategory
  • With a certain complexity

Prompt Localization

When you create your prompt, it should be localized to the language and locale you are working in. For example:

  1. Write the prompt in your native language (NOT English)
  2. Make sure the question or topic is likely to be asked or discussed by native speakers of this language / your locale
  3. Do not mix your language with English, especially in modern terms (e.g. Internet slang, finance terms, etc.)
  4. Do not use literal translations of English into your language

Also reference the tips for building an effective prompt in the drop down section below to help you brainstorm great prompts.

Prompt Categorization

When creating your prompt, you will be given a main Category and Subcategory that the prompt must adhere to. For example:

  • Category: Recommendations / Brainstorming
  • Subcategory: Travel & destinations suggestions
  • Prompt: Can you suggest some must-visit cities around the world known for their unique attractions, diverse cultures, and fascinating experiences?

The drop down list below includes the full list of categories and subcategories that you may encounter on this project.

For inspiration, please reference APPENDIX 1 (at the bottom of these instructions) for example prompts in each category and subcategory. These examples will also be provided in the task. Do NOT use these examples when creating your own prompts.

Prompt Complexity

When creating your prompt(s), please make sure that your prompts have varying levels of difficulty following the complexity explanation below. The complexity of a prompt is going to be measured by the number of constraints you include in your prompt. Low complexity (Easy) prompts will have 0 or 1 constraints while High complexity (Hard) prompts will have more than 5 constraints. Some example constraints are:

  • Make your response 50 words long (format constraint)
  • Respond to me as if you were Superman (style constraint)
  • Only include hotels that are walking distance to a large public park (content constraint)

Additional constraints guide: Flamingo Multilingual Constraints Guide

Again, please create the prompt in the language team you are working in. DO NOT make the prompt in English.

Avoid using unrealistic constraints. These are constraints that a real user would not realistically ask a chatbot. For example:

  • Include a word with the letter R in every sentence.
  • Dont start any sentences with the word The.

Step 2: Selecting the Better Response

Read through the responses provided by both chatbots and select which response is better

When reading each response, keep the following criteria in mind:

  • Accuracy: is the information factual, verifiable, and directly answers the questions from the prompt?
  • Completeness: how well does this response follow the instructions from the prompt?
  • Fluency: how fluent is the response in your language? If there are grammatical errors, then fluency needs to be penalized.
  • Localization (e.g. correct tone, choice of words, slangs): is the response appropriate for your locale? How well do the responses reflect an appropriately conversational tone as an easy to understand, natural-sounding response in your languages typical style?
  • Formatting: is the response well formatted, organized, and easy to read?

Refer to the rating rubric (Appendix 2) for more detail.

Once you have read both of the responses. You will select which response is better following the distribution below.

Model A is much better

Model A is better

Model A is slightly better

Model A is negligibly better

Model B is negligibly better

Model B is slightly better

Model B is better

Model B is much better

How to select the appropriate preference rating:

  • Model [X] is much better: select this option when this response is by far better than the other response (e.g. the other response is in another language, gibberish, had no output, punt, etc.). You should also select this option if the response was better in all of the rating criteria.
  • Model [X] is better: select this option when this response is better than the other in most of the below rating criteria. Select this option especially if most of the important criteria (accuracy, completeness, fluency, or localization) are better than the other response.
  • Model [X] is slightly better: select this option when one or two criteria are better in this response than the other.
  • Model [X] is negligibly better: select this response when the two are basically the same, but you have a slight preference for one over the other.

You will also be asked about the quality of the responses (are they written in your local language & are they understandable). If both responses are bad then mark down Yes (both responses are bad). Both responses are BAD if they:

  • are written in English,
  • are written in another language,
  • are incomprehensible (gibberish), or if
  • are both hallucinations.

If at least one response is good, mark down No (at least one response is good). The purpose of this question is to know if the responses are:

  1. written in your local language
  2. good responses and easy to understand (not gibberish)
  3. not hallucinations

Step 3: Rating the Better Response

Once you have selected the better response, you will need to rate the response along a set of provided dimensions.

Please familiarize yourself with the rating rubric (Appendix 2) for more detail. High-level instructions are below:

You will be asked to critique the model according to the same criteria you used to select the better response. As a reminder, the criteria are as follows:

  • Accuracy: is the information factual, verifiable, and directly answers the questions from the prompt?
  • Completeness: how well does this response follow the instructions from the prompt?
  • Fluency: how fluent is the response in your language? If there are grammatical errors, then fluency needs to be penalized.
  • Localization (e.g. correct tone, choice of words, slangs): is the response appropriate for your locale? How well do the responses reflect an appropriately conversational tone as an easy to understand, natural-sounding response in your languages typical style?
  • Formatting: is the response well formatted, organized, and easy to read?

For Accuracy, make sure to check EVERY FACT AND URL. This is the only way to know how accurate the response is. If your region has a regional search engine (e.g.: if youre looking up Chinese terms, search with Baidu) use that. Otherwise searching on Google or another major search engine is preferred. For any fact, make sure that the details are correct. For URLs, make sure it both works and is relevant to the information in the response.

For each of the above criteria you will determine if there are no issues, minor issues, or major issues.

  • No issues: the response was perfect with respect to the given criteria
  • Minor issues: the response was not perfect and had a few errors with respect to the given criteria
  • Major issues: the response was very off and had more than a few mistakes with respect to the given criteria

Please reference Appendix 3, below, for good and bad examples for each of the above criteria!

Finally, you should write a sentence or two justifying your choices, especially in those areas where the model did poorly and provide examples from the text to support your justification. Please make your responses thoughtful.

Step 4: Editing and Improving the Better Response

Finally, you will be asked to make edits to the portions of the response that you thought could be improved. Based on the limitations you noted above, you should make an effort to fix the parts of the response you thought could be improved.

For example, you could improve the model by:

  • Fact-checking and fixing inaccurate statements
  • Adding sentences to the response that did not address part of the prompt
  • Improving the fluency of the response in your language
  • Fixing grammatical errors in the response
  • Localizing the response if there are places to do so
  • Organize the formatting of the response to make it easier to read or understand

In general, you should be spending no more than 3-4 minutes editing the response. These should be minor edits only, and not a complete rewrite.

Once you are done with your edits, you can submit the task!

Rubric for Reviewers

Important Reminders

  1. DO NOT EDIT THE PROMPT. This will change the responses. You can edit anything else but leave the prompt as is.
  2. Contributors are not allowed to use external LLMs (such as ChatGPT or Gemini) to generate text or get ideas.
  3. Prompts and responses should be written from the perspective of YOU (a human) and should not mention any real-world chatbots, including (but not limited to) ChatGPT, Microsoft Copilot, Bard, Gemini, OpenAI, Perplexity, YouChat, Chatsonic, Socrates.ai, or HuggingChat. (Its okay to mention Google or Bing as search engines, but not as chatbots).
  4. It is OK if the responses contain URLs. We just need to make sure that we fact check that the links exist and are accurate.

Providing Feedback

  • Even if the feedback is minor, the best way to help contributors improve their tasking capabilities is to provide them with thoughtful guidance on how you would have fixed the task. Some examples include:
  • Explaining why you would have selected a different likert score and why
  • Where you think they might need clarification on the dimensional ratings
  • How they can improve their justifications
  • If you are rating the task as (1) Poor / Unacceptable or (2) Bad, the contributor would really benefit from thoughtful feedback. If you think the contributor is a spammer, then do not waste your time providing thoughtful feedback. However, if they just seem to not understand the task at all, providing solid feedback on the most important parts of the task might be very helpful for allowing them to improve on future tasks.

One interesting and complex problem that can be addressed through mathematical modeling in traffic flow is the optimization of traffic light timings at a busy intersection to minimize overall wait times and reduce congestion. This problem involves various dynamic components, including the influx of vehicles, differing vehicle types, pedestrian crossings, and potentially public transportation considerations. Here's a detailed formulation of this problem:

Problem Statement: Optimize Traffic Light Timings at a Busy Intersection

Objective: Minimize the total wait time of vehicles at an intersection during peak traffic hours, while ensuring pedestrian safety and accommodating public transportation schedules.

Variables to Consider:

Lij: The length of time the traffic light is green for flow from direction i to direction j.

Vi: The volume of traffic approaching the intersection from direction i per unit time.

Pi: The pedestrian traffic crossing the intersection from direction i.

T: The total cycle time of the traffic lights (sum of all green, yellow, and red phases).

Sij: The saturation flow rate, or the maximum rate of vehicles that can pass through the intersection from direction i to direction j under green light conditions.

B: Bus and public transportation schedules influencing traffic light priority.

Appendix 2: Project Rating Rubric (Dimensional Rating)

Criteria

Major issues

Minor issues

No issues

Accuracy

The extent to which the information presented is accurate, reliable, and aligns with established facts or evidence.

  • Accuracy of Claims - there are facts which are objectively verifiable as incorrect
  • Misleading Information - Conclusive, common-knowledge counterfactual information can be found via a Google search

  • Accuracy of Claims - there may be some inconsequential facts that are incorrect
  • Misleading Information - presents information that is controversial as fact without giving adequate contextualization

  • Accuracy of Claims - all facts which are objectively verifiable are correct
  • Misleading Information - all claims are appropriately contextualized where necessary and opinions/controversial statements are not stated as fact

If Accuracy is not applicable (e.g. for a creative writing prompt), mark as No Issues

Completeness

The extent to which the response addresses the instructions in the prompt.

  • Prompt Request Coverage - multiple or all of the instructions from the prompt are not followed
  • Relevance - response is not at all relevant to the prompt

  • Prompt Request Coverage - the response follows the most important instructions, but may not address secondary instructions or less critical constraints
  • Relevance - some of the response is not directly relevant to the prompt

  • Prompt Request Coverage - All prompt instructions are followed
  • Relevance - All included information is relevant in responding to the prompt or adds helpful and enriching elements relevant to the task

Fluency

The correctness of the spelling and grammatical structure, and the quality of sentence structure.

  • Readability - The response has multiple spelling or grammatical errors that significantly impact the readability - how easily the response can be parsed by human readers
  • Language Mechanics - Response includes errors that may encompass sentence structure problems, run-on sentences, punctuation/sentence fragments
  • Outline and Coherence - The ideas covered in the response are not outlined or presented in a logical order. Each paragraph jumps from idea to idea with no logical transition

  • Readability - The response has some spelling or grammatical errors but the response is still readable
  • Language Mechanics - Response indicates proficient use of language mechanics, with only minor corrections
  • Outline and Coherence - The response structure is coherent, but the transition between paragraphs/ideas are a bit hard to follow

  • Readability - The response has no spelling or grammatical errors
  • Language Mechanics - Response indicates advanced use of language mechanics, with no minor corrections
  • Outline and Coherence - The order and presentation of ideas is appropriate and coherent in responding to the prompt

Localization

The inclusion of topics and language (idiomatic expressions, slang, etc.) that are relevant to native speakers of a given locale

  • Localized content - The response includes topics that are obviously not relevant to the language locale
  • Localized style - The response does not read like it was written by a native speaker who is from the language locale, and/or the response uses localized expressions incorrectly

  • Localized content - The response includes some topics do not pertain to the language locale
  • Localized style - The response is missing some localized expressions, but is still passable as written by a native speaker
  • Localized content - Where appropriate, the response includes topics that are relevant to the language locale
  • Localized style - The response reads like it was written by a native speaker who is from the language locale (expressions, idioms, slang, cultural norms etc.)

If Localization is not applicable (e.g. for a math prompt), mark as No Issues

Formatting

The presentation of the written response, including elements such as structure, layout, and visual clarity. Appropriate use of bullets, numbering, italics, etc. Also look for paragraph formatting and the visual separation of ideas.

  • Visual Processing - Formatting is a barrier to processing the information quickly. There is no visual separation between ideas; Distinct ideas are lumped together in one paragraph; Does not use formatting elements (list, tables, bullets, headers, etc.) appropriately

  • Visual Processing - there are one or two ways in which the formatting could have been improved but it is not a significant obstacle to processing the information quickly
  • Visual Processing - The formatting elements used allow the reader to clearly follow the information provided (where appropriate uses lists, tables, titles, bullets, etc. to present information in an easy-to-process way)
